CSS Positioning Examples

Static Positioning

Elements with the default positioning (static) are placed in the normal document flow. They do not respond to top, bottom, left, or right properties. Changes made to other elements don't affect statically positioned elements.

Static

Relative Positioning

Elements with relative positioning are positioned relative to their normal position in the document flow. They can be moved using top, bottom, left, or right properties without affecting other elements.

Relative
Absolute

Absolute Positioning

Elements with absolute positioning are removed from the normal document flow and positioned relative to their closest positioned ancestor (or the body if no positioned ancestor exists). They won't affect the layout of other elements.

Absolute

Fixed Positioning

Elements with fixed positioning are positioned relative to the browser window. They remain in the same place even if the page is scrolled. Commonly used for headers, footers, or elements that should remain visible regardless of scrolling.

Fixed

Sticky Positioning

Elements with sticky positioning are positioned based on the user's scroll position. They act like relative positioning until they reach a specific point on the screen (defined by top, bottom, left, or right), then they stick ("freeze") at that position.

Sticky
